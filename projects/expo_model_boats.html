Overview

The purpose of this project was to design and fabricate small-scale demonstration models that reflect the basic architecture of a solar-powered hydrofoil racing boat — including components such as the hull, deck, struts with foils, propulsion system, and photovoltaic panel. These models were developed as part of the AGH Solar Boat initiative for use at the Osaka Expo 2025 as well as in showcases and technical presentations.

The main educational goal was to demonstrate the conversion of solar energy into mechanical motion by driving a small propeller mounted on a small DC motor directly from a solar panel. The models were designed to be visually appealing, mechanically simple, and easy to assemble or mass-produce for hands-on demonstrations. They also serve as meaningful gifts for sponsors and partners.

An extended version of the model included a miniature propulsion system and custom PCB with onboard electronics based on an ESP32 Wi-Fi microcontroller, allowing for remote steering via a web interface. These interactive boats were designed to operate in environments such as small indoor water tanks, making them ideal for event booths and educational workshops.

Technical Highlights

  • What you did / implemented

    • The project began in response to a proposal by the AGH Solar Boat supervisor to prepare workshops for Osaka Expo 2025. I proposed the idea of creating small, modular, visually appealing demonstration boats equipped with photovoltaic panels and a small DC motor.

    I determined the appropriate size of the model to ensure it would be compatible with my 3D printer and the selected miniature coreless DC motors and PV panels. I also verified that the motor could be powered directly from the panel under a strong light source.

    I developed a CAD model and iteratively refined it through rapid prototyping to optimize the geometry, dimensions, and component fits. I then manufactured several units of the basic demonstration boat.

    To enable remote control and water operation, I selected the electronics: an ESP32, H-bridge motor driver, steering servo, and a Li-Ion battery. I first built a prototype on a universal soldering board and designed a proper PCB in EasyEDA.

    The PCB included INA229 ICs for monitoring battery and PV voltages, a basic DC-DC converter for the panel, an SSD1306 OLED display, and protective elements for safe battery operation.

    I wrote the firmware and a web interface for the ESP32-based control. I also updated the mechanical model to accommodate the servo, included a bearing for the steering strut, and integrated the battery and PCB inside the hull.

    The complete model was tested in water during trials of the Solar Boat team’s autonomous vessel.

  • Interesting challenge
    • A key challenge was integrating multiple functions—motor control, PV monitoring, battery protection, and remote interface—into a single compact PCB while keeping the form factor suitable for a small model boat. Mechanically, the biggest issue was designing a steering system small and precise enough to fit into the hull, including a reliable bearing and linkage to the rudder strut. From a firmware perspective, creating a responsive, user-friendly Wi-Fi interface on the ESP32 that could run on constrained hardware was also non-trivial.
  • Technologies used
    • 3D printing (FDM), Fusion 360 for CAD, EasyEDA for PCB design, ESP32 with ESP-IDF and Arduino framework, I2C peripherals (SSD1306, INA229), Li-Ion battery handling and protection, H-bridge DC motor driver, Wi-Fi interface (HTML + JS), and soldered prototyping on perfboard.
